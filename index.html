📖 Grammar Basics

Slovak grammar is more complex than French or English — but understanding a few key ideas will help enormously. Don't try to memorize everything at once!

1. Stress is Always on the First Syllable

Unlike French (where stress falls at the end) or English (which is unpredictable), Slovak stress is simple: the first syllable of every word is always stressed.

🇫🇷 Pour les francophones: En français vous accentuez la dernière syllabe; en slovaque c'est toujours la première syllabe. Essayez: DO-brý, ĎA-ku-jem.
ĎA-ku-jem / DO-brý deň / KRÁ-sna
Thank you / Good day / Beautiful
Merci / Bonjour / Belle

2. Nouns Have Genders

Like French, Slovak nouns are masculine, feminine, or neuter. This affects adjectives, verbs, and word endings.

🇫🇷 Bonne nouvelle! Le concept de genre grammatical est familier aux francophones. Masculin = «muž» (homme), Féminin = «žena» (femme), Neutre = «mesto» (ville).
dobrý muž · dobrá žena · dobré mesto
a good man · a good woman · a good city
un bon homme · une bonne femme · une bonne ville

3. Cases — The Big Challenge

Slovak has 7 grammatical cases — word endings change depending on the noun's role in the sentence. English has none; French has almost none. This is the hardest part!

⚠️ Don't panic! You don't need to master all 7 cases to communicate. Start by recognizing patterns, not memorizing tables. Even Slovaks struggle to name all cases.
Vidím ženu. / Hovorím so ženou. / To je žena.
I see the woman. / I'm talking with the woman. / That is the woman.
Je vois la femme. / Je parle avec la femme. / C'est la femme.

4. No Articles (No «le», «la», «the»)

Slovak has no articles — no equivalent of "the", "a", "le", "la", or "un". The noun stands alone.

🇫🇷 Pour les francophones: Vous n'avez pas besoin de dire «le» ou «un» — c'est beaucoup plus simple! «Pes» = le chien / un chien / des chiens selon le contexte.
Pes je veľký.
The dog is big. / A dog is big.
Le chien est grand.

5. Verb "To Be" — Byť

The verb "to be" is essential. Here are its present tense forms:

ja som · ty si · on/ona je
my sme · vy ste · oni sú
I am · you are · he/she is
we are · you (pl.) are · they are
je suis · tu es · il/elle est
nous sommes · vous êtes · ils/elles sont

6. Useful Question Words

Kto? · Čo? · Kde? · Kedy? · Prečo? · Ako? · Koľko?
Who? · What? · Where? · When? · Why? · How? · How many?
Qui? · Quoi? · Où? · Quand? · Pourquoi? · Comment? · Combien?

7. Aký / Aká / Aké / Ako — "What kind of?"

This word asks about the quality or type of something — like "what kind?" or "what is it like?". It changes its ending to match the gender of the noun it refers to.

🇫🇷 Pour les francophones: C'est comme «quel / quelle» en français — il s'accorde avec le nom. «Aký» avec un masculin, «aká» avec un féminin, «aké» avec un neutre. Et «ako» est l'adverbe — comme «comment» ou «comme».
Aký je ten film? — Aký muž? (masculine)
Aká je tá kniha? — Aká žena? (feminine)
Aké je to mesto? — Aké auto? (neuter)
Ako sa voláš? — Ako sa máš? (adverb — how?)
What is the film like? — What kind of man? (m.)
What is the book like? — What kind of woman? (f.)
What is the city like? — What kind of car? (n.)
What is your name? — How are you? (how = adverb)
Comment est ce film? — Quel homme? (m.)
Comment est ce livre? — Quelle femme? (f.)
Comment est cette ville? — Quelle voiture? (n.)
Comment tu t'appelles? — Comment tu vas? (adverbe)
💡 Key rule: Aký / aká / aké = asking about a noun (agree with gender). Ako = adverb, never changes. «Ako si?» = How are you? «Aký je?» = What is he/it like?

8. Ten / Tá / To vs. Tamten / Tamtá / Tamto — "This / That / That over there"

Slovak makes a distinction between pointing at something nearby or in general (ten/tá/to) and pointing at something further away (tamten/tamtá/tamto). But in everyday speech, ten/tá/to is used most of the time — even for things far away — because context makes it clear.

🇫🇷 Pour les francophones: Pensez à «ce/cet/cette» pour ten/tá/to et «celui-là / celle-là» pour tamten/tamtá/tamto. Mais en slovaque courant, on utilise presque toujours ten/tá/to — exactement comme en français où «ce» suffit dans la plupart des situations.
ten muž / tá žena / to auto (masculine / feminine / neuter)
tamten muž / tamtá žena / tamto auto (that one over there)

Pozri sa na ten strom. (Look at that tree — nearby or just pointing)
Nie tento, tamten! (Not this one, that one over there!)
that man / that woman / that car (general pointing)
that man over there / that woman over there / that car over there

Look at that tree. (nearby or general)
Not this one, that one over there!
cet homme / cette femme / cette voiture (pointage général)
cet homme là-bas / cette femme là-bas / cette voiture là-bas

Regarde cet arbre. (proche ou général)
Pas celui-ci, celui-là!
💡 Practical tip: In everyday Slovak, people almost always use ten / tá / to. You only use tamten / tamtá / tamto when you are specifically contrasting two things — "not this one, that one over there." If in doubt, use ten / tá / to and you will almost always be correct.

9. The Rhythmic Law — Why malá but krásna?

This is one of the most surprising things about Slovak — and it's completely phonetic. Slovak has a natural rule: two long syllables cannot follow each other in a row. So when an adjective stem already contains a long vowel (á, é, í, ý, ú, ô), the feminine ending shortens from -á to -a.

🇫🇷 Pour les francophones: C'est une règle de rythme naturel — comme en musique, deux syllabes longues de suite sonnent lourd en slovaque, donc la langue «respire» en raccourcissant la terminaison. Ce n'est pas une exception à apprendre — c'est une loi phonétique qui s'applique automatiquement.
🎵 The rule in simple terms: If the adjective stem has a long syllable → ending shortens. If the stem is all short → ending stays long (-á).
krásna žena · krásna kniha (stem KRÁS- is long → ending = short -a)
malá žena · malá kniha (stem MA- is short → ending = long -á)
múdra žena (stem MÚD- is long → ending = short -a)
mladá žena (stem MLA- is short → ending = long -á)
beautiful woman / book (long stem → short ending)
small woman / book (short stem → long ending)
wise woman (long stem → short ending)
young woman (short stem → long ending)
belle femme / livre (radical long → terminaison courte)
petite femme / livre (radical court → terminaison longue)
femme intelligente (radical long → terminaison courte)
jeune femme (radical court → terminaison longue)
Adjective (feminine) Stem has long vowel? Ending
krásna Yes — krás- (á is long) -a (short)
malá No — mal- (all short) -á (long)
múdra Yes — múd- (ú is long) -a (short)
mladá No — mlad- (all short) -á (long)
pekná No — pekn- (all short) -á (long)
⚠️ Exception — animal adjectives (vzor páví): Adjectives derived from animal names do NOT follow the rhythmic law — they keep their long endings in all forms, even when the stem already has a long syllable. Páví (peacock's) is the model example, but this applies to all animal adjectives: líščí (fox's), vtáčí (bird's), motýlí (butterfly's), kohútí (rooster's), býčí (bull's), and so on. So if you see an adjective ending in -í that describes something belonging to an animal, expect long vowels throughout — the rhythmic law simply doesn't apply to this group.
🎵 Memory trick: Say both versions out loud — krásná feels awkward and heavy. Krásna flows naturally. Your ear will start to guide you automatically after some practice — just like a native speaker!

💡 Tips for Learning Slovak

Learning Slovak as a French or English speaker is a real challenge — but also a beautiful adventure. Here are the best strategies to make progress faster.

🎧
Listen before you speak
Slovak pronunciation is very different from French or English. Listen to Slovak music, podcasts, or YouTube videos before trying to speak. Apps like Pimsleur or YouTube channels for Slovak learners are excellent starting points.
📖
Reading is easier than it sounds
Slovak spelling is phonetic — every letter has ONE sound, always. Once you know the alphabet, you can read any Slovak word out loud correctly. Unlike French or English, there are no silent letters and no confusing spelling rules.
🔤
Focus on the special letters first
Learn š, č, ž, ľ, ď, ť, ň — these are the letters most different from French/English. The good news: Š = French «ch», Ž = French «j», Č = «tch». You already know these sounds from French!
🗣️
Don't be afraid to make mistakes
Slovaks are always delighted and touched when foreigners try to speak Slovak. Even a simple «Dobrý deň» or «Ďakujem» will make people smile warmly. Mistakes are part of learning — embrace them!
💬
Start with phrases, not grammar
Slovak grammar is complex. Don't start with textbooks and grammar tables — start with practical phrases for everyday situations. Grammar understanding will come naturally with exposure and practice over time.
🇨🇿
Czech is very similar!
Czech and Slovak are so close that speakers can usually understand each other. If you ever find Slovak content too rare, Czech movies, music, and apps (like Duolingo for Czech) will help you enormously — and transfer directly to Slovak.
📱
Useful Apps & Resources
Duolingo — no Slovak, but Czech is very close. Anki — make flashcard decks for Slovak words. Google Translate — has Slovak with audio pronunciation. YouTube — search "Slovak for beginners" for free video lessons.
❤️
Your best teacher is your motivation
Learning a language to connect with someone you love is one of the most powerful motivations. Even small steps — learning a greeting, a phrase, a sweet word — mean the world to someone from Slovakia. Každý krôčik sa počíta. (Every little step counts.)
